Laser Tracking Robot

Laser Tracking Robot

The objective of this project was to model and control a robotic system. The group designed a robot control algorithm that used sensory feedback in closed-loop form to perform a functional behavior or task.

The final product was this laser tracking robot. The 2 DOF robotic arm uses a mounted, fixed camera to track the current system's orientation via the red laser dot from its end effector. Reading an external input of a desired location represented by a green laser pointer dot, the system takes this desired position in pixel space and translates this to motion in servo space to align the visual cues using PID control.

  • Class: MAE 298, UCLA
  • Year: Spring 2016

Tunable Single-Axis Tilt MEMS Mirror

Tunable Single-Axis Tilt MEMS Mirror

A flexure-based micro-mirror that can tip back and forth rapidly about a single rotational axis can be used to steer light for a variety of high-impact precision applications (e.g., optical switches and scanning systems). For high-speed scenarios, such mirrors are often driven to operate at their resonant frequencies to minimize the system’s required actuation energy and to simplify the mirror’s control such that no sensors or closed-loop control circuitry is necessary to achieve the desired speed. To achieve a tunable range of resonant frequencies, this micro-mirror system was designed with an additional set of actuators that could independently tune the system’s rotational resonant frequency by deforming its flexure elements.

CAD models were generated with SolidWorks, resonant frequency analysis was performed in SolidWorks and Matlab, and results were confirmed with simulation in MSC Adams.

  • Class: MAE 294, UCLA
  • Year: Winter 2016

Connecting Rod Design & Analysis

Connecting Rod Design & Analysis

Connecting rod designed for use in 2009 Mini Cooper engine. The following procedure was used to create and refine a final design:

1. Load Analysis
2. Preliminary Design using Beam Theory
3. Two-Dimensional Solid Model
4. Two-Dimensional Elastic Finite Element Analysis
5. Lifetime Analysis: fatigue
6. Lifetime Analysis: fracture
7. Design Optimization
8. Three-Dimensional Solid Model
9. Three-Dimensional Elastic Finite Element Analysis

The design was completed and refined using fatigue and fracture analysis methods with the aid of SolidWorks, COMSOL, and Abaqus.

  • Class: MAE 296A, UCLA
  • Year: Fall 2015

Gearbox Design

Gearbox Design

The objective was to design a gearbox that met the following requirements:

• Gear ratio of 1/240
• Orthogonal input and output shafts
• Supports 50 lb. overhung load at the output shaft
• Support input shaft rotation of 5000 rpm
• Minimum lifetime of 25,000 hours

In order to maximize ease of manufacturing, stock parts were used when possible.

Parts and assemblies were made using Pro/E (Creo)

  • Class: ME 359, Boston University
  • Year: 2014

Catheterization Lab Project

Catheterization procedures in the U.S. currently total 4.5M yearly, and this number is projected increase to 4.68M by 2025 as the baby boomer generation ages (51% of catheterization procedures currently are performed on patients aged 65+). As popularity of these procedures increases, the incidence of reported back pain during the procedures becomes a growing concern.

As part of an interdisciplinary team of business and product design professionals, I am working to create a product that will ensure that patients are receiving the best clinical treatment experience possible by minimizing discomfort during catheterization procedures.

  • Year: Winter and Spring 2016
